The deposit-and-trade ladder

Of your participants, how many climb each tier — and how many fall away. This drop-off is the single biggest driver of cost.

In plain words

The promo has three steps, or tiers. Not everyone climbs all the way: some do the first step and stop, some go further, and a few finish everything. These percentages say how many people reach each step.

Formula

total participants × that tier’s reach % = people who reach it
Reach Tier 1: 20 × 72% = 14
Reach Tier 2: 20 × 45% = 9
Finish all 3: 20 × 30% = 6

Common questions

  • Why do the percentages only go down as you climb?Because you can’t reach step 2 without doing step 1. Each higher step is a smaller group sitting inside the one below it. The tool won’t even let us set it any other way, on purpose.
  • Where are these percentages from?Assumptions again, our best guess at drop-off. The sliders let us test an optimistic version and a pessimistic one.

From "reached" to "stopped at"

In plain words

The ladder counts how high people climbed, so the groups overlap: someone who finished is also counted in Tier 1 and Tier 2. To work out how much they traded, we count each person once, grouped by where they stopped. So from each tier’s “reached” count, we take away the people who climbed higher, leaving just the ones who stopped there.

Formula

stopped at a tier = reached it − the ones who climbed past it
Stopped at Tier 1: 14 − 9 = 5
Stopped at Tier 2: 9 − 6 = 3
Finished (top): 6 − 0 = 6

Common questions

  • Why do we need this extra step?Because the ladder double-counts on purpose: a finisher shows up on every rung they passed. If we used those numbers to work out trading, we’d count the same person several times. This strips it back so each person counts once, where they stopped.
  • Why is the finished number the same as in Stage 3?Because the top is the end of the road. Nobody climbs higher than the last tier, so there’s no one to take away. Everyone who reached the top also stopped at the top, so that number carries straight through.

STAGE 4

Trading volume

Each cohort trades the lots their tier requires. Completers trade the most, and there are the most of them — so they drive the volume.

In plain words

Think of a coffee shop loyalty card, where you collect a stamp each time you buy a coffee. To reach each level of the promo, people had to collect a set number of stamps: a few for the first level, more for the next, and the most to finish. We add up all the stamps everyone collected for the grand total, then take a bit off for any that weren’t really earned, and what’s left is the trading we can rely on. (In trading, one stamp is called a “lot”, just a standard unit of how much someone trades.)

Formula

gross lots = each group × its lot target, added up
(5 × 2) + (3 × 5) + (6 × 10) = 85

effective lots = gross − leakage shave
85 − 8% ≈ 78

Common questions

  • What’s a “lot”?It’s just the standard unit we measure trade size in. One lot is a fixed amount; ten lots is ten times that.
  • What’s the shave for?Some people try to game promos: opening and closing trades just to rack up volume, or hedging both directions so they take no real risk. We don’t want to count those, so we knock off a safety margin. The 8% is an estimate, and Risk will tighten it.

STAGE 5

Trading revenue

The effective lots finally turn into money — multiplied by profit per lot, which depends on who's trading and what they trade.

In plain words

This is the money the company makes from clients trading. Each time someone trades, the company keeps a small slice, like a phone shop keeping about $30 on a $1,000 phone, win or lose. We take the reliable trading from Stage 4 and multiply it by that slice. It’s kept low on purpose: there’s extra the company can sometimes make that we don’t count here, so the real figure is likely a bit higher.

Formula

effective lots × earning per lot = trading revenue
78 × $4.90 ≈ $383

Common questions

  • Where’s the $4.90 per lot from?It’s the average we earn per lot across the products in the promo, from the Risk team’s profit-per-lot sheet. Some products earn more, some less, and this is the blended average. The mix dial lets us test what happens if clients trade cheaper or richer products than we assumed.
  • Is this everything the company makes?No, it’s the safe, guaranteed slice only. There’s a second, bumpier way the company can earn that we leave out on purpose, so this stays a careful, low figure and the real amount is likely higher, never lower.

How it works. Think of a phone shop. When it sells a $1,000 phone, it only keeps a small part as its own profit, maybe $30, and the rest just pays for the phone itself. The company here is the same. Each time a client trades, it keeps a small slice as profit, like the shop’s $30. Whether that client’s trade wins or loses, the company keeps its slice anyway. Add up that little slice across every trade made in the campaign, and you get this number.

Why it’s set low on purpose. The company can sometimes earn more on top of this, but that extra part is unpredictable, so we don’t count it here. That keeps this a careful, safe figure. The real amount is likely a little higher than this, and never lower.

In plain words

Different products earn the company a different amount each time they’re traded. This table shows the products people can trade in the promo, how much each one earns per lot, and the share of the trading we expect each to get. The “blend” at the bottom is the overall average, weighted so the products people trade more of count for more. That blend is the “earning per lot” figure the revenue above is built on.

Formula

blend = (each product’s profit per lot × its share), added up, then divided by the total share
= $4.8961 per lot, on the Standard account

STAGE 6

What it costs

The other side of the ledger — the media to bring people in, the reward credit they earn, the gold catch, and fixed production.

In plain words

This is what the whole campaign costs, split into four buckets. Media is the money we spend on ads to bring people in. Reward credit is the trading credit we hand out as prizes. Creative is the one-off cost of designing and producing the campaign. And gold is the catch prize for the first few finishers. Add the four together and that’s the total. (These figures are for the lean reward design we agreed on.)

Formula

media + reward credit + creative + gold = total cost
$2,700 + $280 + $3,000 + $394 ≈ $6,374

Common questions

  • How’s the reward credit worked out?Each group earns credit for the steps they finished: stopping at step 1 earns $5, step 2 earns $15, and finishing earns $35. We multiply by how many are in each group and add up. (5 × $5) + (3 × $15) + (6 × $35) = $280.
  • Why is creative a fixed $3,000?Because it’s a one-off production cost (design, assets, setup) that we pay once whether 20 people join or 2,000. That’s why a small campaign struggles to cover it, which you’ll see in the verdict.
  • How’s the gold worked out?Only the first 3 finishers get gold, one gram each. We take the price you keyed in — RM 548.33 a gram — convert it to dollars at 4.14 (about $131 a gram), and multiply by the 3 winners. That’s $394.

STAGE 7

The verdict

Revenue and cost brought together, read two ways: does the trading pay for the campaign, and does it pull in client money?

In plain words

This is the “is it worth it?” check, looked at two ways. First, does the money we earn from the trading cover what the campaign costs? Second, for every $1 we spend, how much client money comes in the door? When both fall short, the campaign loses money on paper, and the only reason to run it would be the longer-term value of the new clients it brings in.

The two headline checks

does the trading pay for it? = (revenue − cost) ÷ cost
($383 − $6,374) ÷ $6,374 = −94%

does it pull money in? = deposits ÷ cost
$2,822 ÷ $6,374 = 0.44×

Every other number on screen

what it costs = media + reward credit + creative + gold
$2,700 + $280 + $3,000 + $394 = $6,374

trading income − cost = revenue − cost
$383 − $6,374 = −$5,991

we earn per lot = revenue ÷ effective lots
$383 ÷ 78 ≈ $4.90

break-even per lot = cost ÷ effective lots
$6,374 ÷ 78 ≈ $81.51

trading covers of cost = revenue ÷ cost
$383 ÷ $6,374 = 6%

What the colours mean

Green is good — that number is working in our favour. Red means we’re losing on that measure, below zero. Amber is a watch-out: not a loss, but under where we’d want it, like the trading covering less than 100% of the cost, or under $1 of deposits for every $1 we spend.

Common questions

  • Why does a small campaign look bad here?Because the fixed costs, the creative and the media, are large when only a few people take part. The trading and the deposits can’t cover them yet. It works at bigger numbers, or once we count what these clients are worth later on.
  • What does “break-even needs per lot” mean?We earn about $4.90 on each lot traded. To cover the whole campaign, each lot would need to earn about $81.51. That gap is the scale problem in a single number.
  • What does “trading covers of cost” mean?It’s the share of the cost that the trading income pays for. Right now that’s about 6%, so the trading pays for a small slice and the rest leans on the deposits and the future value of these clients. The higher it climbs the more the trading pays its own way; at 100% it would cover the whole campaign on its own.
